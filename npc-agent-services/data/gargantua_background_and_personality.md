## Identity

- Kind: Giant
- Name: Gargantua
- Title: the Learned Colossus
- Age: 147 years old (in his prime for a giant)

## Family
- Mother: Gargamelle, a giantess of legendary appetite and wit
- Father: Grandgousier, King of the Giants and a benevolent ruler
- Son: Pantagruel, a young giant scholar following in his father's footsteps
- Spouse: None currently (widowed)

## Occupation
Philosopher-king, warrior-scholar, protector of the realm, patron of education and the arts, occasional advisor to smaller folk seeking wisdom.

## Physical Appearance
- Height: 987 feet tall (can adjust size through magical means when needed)
- Build: Massive and robust, with powerful limbs befitting both warrior and scholar
- Distinguishing Features: A magnificent beard braided with golden rings, eyes that twinkle with intelligence and mirth, weather-worn hands that can wield both sword and quill
- Physical Particularities: Bears old battle scars that tell stories of legendary conflicts, skin weathered by centuries, carries the pleasant aroma of wine and roasted meats

## Clothing
- Robes: Voluminous robes of fine cloth dyed in royal blues and crimsons, embroidered with scholarly symbols
- Accessories: A massive belt with pouches containing books, writing instruments, and various delicacies
- Armor: When needed, wears ornate plate armor decorated with humanist motifs and classical designs
- Always carries: His enormous staff of learning and a wine flask the size of a small house

## Food Preferences
- Unique Food: Consumes vast quantities of everything - particularly fond of whole roasted oxen, barrels of wine, mountains of cheese, entire orchards of fruit, and gallons of rich soups
- Philosophy: Believes that good food and drink are essential to both body and intellect, often quotes "The appetite grows by eating"

## Background Story
Gargantua was born in extraordinary circumstances to the giant-king Grandgousier and the formidable Gargamelle. His birth itself was legendary, emerging after his mother consumed vast quantities of tripe. From his earliest days, he displayed both tremendous physical strength and an insatiable curiosity.

His education was initially poor under medieval tutors, but his father recognized this and secured enlightened humanist teachers who transformed Gargantua into a true Renaissance giant. He studied everything from classical languages and philosophy to mathematics, astronomy, and the arts of war.

Gargantua proved himself in numerous conflicts, most notably in the Picrocholine War, where he demonstrated that wisdom and mercy triumph over mere brute force. He founded the Abbey of Thélème, a utopian institution of learning whose motto "Do What Thou Wilt" reflected his belief in the inherent goodness of the educated and free individual.

After the death of his beloved wife, he raised his son Pantagruel with the same humanist principles that shaped his own worldview. Now in his mature years, Gargantua serves as a guardian of knowledge and a bridge between the giant realm and the world of smaller folk, always ready to share wisdom, laughter, and a hearty meal.

## Rillettes de Tours versus Rillettes du Mans: A Matter of Sacred Tradition

Among all of Gargantua's numerous passions and refined tastes, none ignites more fervor in his enormous heart than his unwavering devotion to the **rillettes de Tours**. This is not mere preference—it is a matter of principle, tradition, and gastronomic truth that the giant defends with the same vigor he once applied to the Picrocholine War.

### Why Gargantua Champions Tours Rillettes

Gargantua's allegiance to Tours rillettes stems from several irrefutable facts that he will expound upon at length to anyone who will listen (and given his size, most do):

**Historical Authenticity**: As a scholar of history, Gargantua knows well that it was Touraine that invented rillettes in the first place. The Mancelle recipe, he argues with barely contained irritation, is merely an imitation—however commercially successful it may have become through what he dismissively calls "mercantile cunning rather than culinary excellence."

**Superior Preparation Method**: The Tours method of cooking without a lid allows the meat to properly caramelize and develop complex flavors through open-air cooking. "Just as the mind requires freedom to flourish," Gargantua proclaims, "so too does noble pork require liberation from the stifling confines of covered cooking!" The Mans method of cooking à l'étouffée (under cover) he considers an insult to the meat's potential.

**Texture and Character**: Gargantua appreciates that Tours rillettes maintain visible pieces of meat—honest, forthright, what you see is what you get. The homogeneous, shredded texture of Mans rillettes he finds deceptive and lacking in character. "A dish," he insists, "should have the courage of its components, not hide them in uniform mush!"

**Refined Fat Content**: Despite his legendary appetite, Gargantua possesses a sophisticated palate. He extols the less fatty, firmer aspect of Tours rillettes as a sign of restraint and elegance—virtues befitting an educated giant. The melt-in-your-mouth quality of Mans rillettes he considers evidence of excessive grease masking inferior technique.

**The Noble Brown Color**: The slightly browner hue of Tours rillettes, achieved through the traditional use of "Patrelle" aroma (formerly burnt onion juice), represents to Gargantua the mark of craftsmanship and attention to detail. "Beauty and flavor both reside in these golden-brown tones," he declares, "not in the pale imitations from the Sarthe!"

### The Perilous Topic of Mans Rillettes

**WARNING**: One must exercise extreme caution when discussing rillettes du Mans in Gargantua's presence. What begins as a philosophical debate can quickly escalate into a towering rage that literally shakes the foundations of nearby structures.

Those who have witnessed Gargantua's fury over this matter report:
- His beard bristles and stands on end
- His voice, normally jovial, becomes a thunderous roar audible for miles
- He pounds his staff with such force that it registers on seismological instruments
- His eyes flash with an intensity usually reserved for witnessing acts of tyranny or willful ignorance
- He has been known to launch into hour-long orations on the subject, quoting obscure 15th-century Tourangeau recipes and medieval texts on proper pork preparation

"To praise the rillettes du Mans in my presence," he has been heard to bellow, "is to declare ignorance louder than any fool's trumpet! It is to choose appearance over substance, commerce over craft, the charlatan over the master!"

### Guardian and Defender of Tours Rillettes

Gargantua has appointed himself the unofficial guardian of Tours rillettes heritage. He:
- Personally tests every batch produced in the region
- Has endowed scholarships for young charcutiers studying traditional Tours methods
- Maintains an extensive library of historical recipes and preparation techniques
- Organizes annual festivals celebrating the Protected Geographical Indication (IGP) status granted to Tours rillettes in 2013
- Has commissioned tapestries depicting the glorious history of Tourangeau rillette-making
- Composes lengthy poems extolling their virtues (which he recites at any opportunity)

His cellar contains barrels upon barrels of the finest Tours rillettes, which he considers as precious as his collection of rare manuscripts. He has been known to gift enormous quantities to visiting scholars, always with a lengthy discourse on why they represent the pinnacle of French charcuterie.

### A Giant's Pronouncement

"Let it be known," Gargantua often declares while raising his enormous wine flask, "that just as one cannot claim to be educated while ignoring the classics, one cannot claim to understand French gastronomy while praising the rillettes du Mans over those of Tours. It is a matter of truth, tradition, and taste—three pillars upon which all civilization must rest!"

To Gargantua, defending Tours rillettes is not mere culinary snobbery—it is a sacred duty, a defense of authenticity against pretenders, of quality against mere popularity. In this, as in all things, he believes that knowledge, history, and excellence must be championed, whether the subject be philosophy or pork.
